Artemis 2 — Orion's first crewed lunar mission

Artemis 2 is NASA's first crewed flight of the Orion spacecraft beyond low Earth orbit since Apollo 17 in 1972. Unlike a landing mission, Orion does not orbit the Moon — it loops around the far side on a free-return trajectory and uses lunar gravity to slingshot back to Earth. This document walks through the real mission and then explains how each step is recreated in the simulation preset 2Artemis.json.

Artemis 2 is unique among lunar missions because Orion never enters lunar orbit. Instead it executes a circumlunar free-return: a trajectory chosen so that, even with no further engine firings, the Moon's gravity will deflect the spacecraft back toward Earth on its own. It is the safest possible flight path for a crewed test, and is also the cheapest in propellant.

The six mission phases

1
Launch

SLS ascent to LEO

The Space Launch System lifts off from Kennedy Space Center. The core stage and solid boosters burn out, then the Interim Cryogenic Propulsion Stage (ICPS) places Orion in a low parking orbit around Earth at roughly 185 × 1,800 km.

2
HEO checkout

High Earth Orbit shakedown

Unique to Artemis 2 — before going to the Moon, the ICPS performs a perigee raise that puts Orion in a highly elliptical orbit (perigee ~185 km, apogee ~71,000 km). Over ~24 hours, the crew checks every system: life support, manual piloting, proximity-operations rendezvous practice with the spent ICPS, deep-space comms. If anything fails, Orion stays Earth-bound. This phase has no analog in Apollo and is the safety hallmark of Artemis 2.

3
TLI

Trans-Lunar Injection

At HEO perigee — moving fastest, deepest in Earth's gravity well — Orion fires the ICPS one final time. This single burn (~3.05 km/s of Δv) adds the energy needed to reach lunar distance. Orion separates and coasts toward the Moon for about 4 days.

4
Flyby

Lunar far-side encounter

Orion does not brake into orbit. It passes ~10,300 km above the far side, and the Moon's gravity bends its path through roughly 180° back toward Earth. From the crew's perspective they become the humans farthest from Earth in history (~400,000 km).

5
Free return

Coast back to Earth

About 4–5 more days of coasting. Small mid-course corrections may refine the entry corridor, but no major burns are needed — the trajectory was designed to land Orion at Earth from the moment TLI fired.

6
Re-entry

Atmospheric entry & splashdown

Orion's service module is jettisoned. The crew capsule re-enters at ~11 km/s, ablates through peak heating (~2,800 °C), deploys parachutes, and splashes down in the Pacific Ocean off the California coast.

Free return — the key idea. A free-return trajectory is one where, after the trans-lunar injection burn, no further propulsion is required to come back. The TLI is aimed slightly past the Moon, not at it; the Moon's gravity then acts as a natural U-turn. Apollo 8, 10, and 11 used variants of this; Apollo 13 famously fell back on a free-return after the explosion that disabled its main engine.

2 · The Simulation — 2Artemis.json

The preset stages a single rocket, Artemis 2 (Orion), beginning landed on Earth, with a queue of 31 maneuvers that fly the entire mission autonomously. The 2D N-body simulator means the Moon's gravity is always acting on the spacecraft — so a real free-return is physically possible, not faked.

Why the MCC is the whole point. Without step 18, wait_for_transfer + auto_transfer compute a Hohmann ellipse arriving at the Moon's exact position at the exact time the Moon is there — a guaranteed collision. The MCC nudges the trajectory just enough that Orion reaches lunar distance offset from the Moon's centre, the Moon's gravity well grabs it, and the free-return geometry takes care of the rest. In real Artemis 2 the TLI itself already aims past the Moon — same physics, same outcome.

3 · Real ↔ Simulation

Each phase of the real mission maps to one or more maneuvers in the queue. The simulation is 2D and uses gameplay-tuned units, but the orbital mechanics are faithful: the same trajectory shape, the same reliance on lunar gravity to do the U-turn, the same checkout-before-TLI architecture.

Real Artemis 2 phaseSimulation stepsFidelity notes
SLS launch → LEO Steps 1–6 (ascend, kill vertical, rotate, sideways burn, circularize) The sim splits the ascent into discrete burns. Real SLS does it in one continuous gravity turn, but the end state — clean circular LEO — is identical.
HEO checkout (24 h) Steps 7–13 (HEO burn, coast to apogee, coast back, re-circularize) The signature Artemis 2 feature. The sim raises apogee, completes one full elliptical loop, then trims back to LEO before TLI — mirroring the real checkout architecture.
TLI at HEO perigee Steps 14–16 (re-circularize, wait for phase, auto_transfer) Real TLI is the ICPS firing for ~18 minutes. The sim folds the impulse into one Hohmann burn computed by the autopilot.
Aim past the Moon Step 18 (MCC nudge) In reality, the offset is baked into the TLI targeting. In the sim, auto_transfer always aims at the body centre, so we add a separate small burn to shift the aim point.
4-day translunar coast Step 20 (wait_for_altitude 300 ↑) Time is compressed via the speed multiplier; the physics (gravity from Earth and Moon) runs at full fidelity throughout.
Lunar flyby + gravity assist Embedded in steps 20–22 The Moon's gravity bends the trajectory automatically — no scripted maneuver. This is the genuinely Newtonian moment of the simulation.
Free-return coast Step 22 (wait_for_altitude 250 ↓) Pure coast. Demonstrates that no engine firing is required to come back — the trajectory was set at TLI.
Re-entry & splashdown Steps 23–31 (approach, deorbit, final descent, circularize, radial-out) The sim has no atmosphere or parachutes, so the heat-shielded entry is replaced by a propulsive braking sequence ending in a vertical soft landing.
What you're seeing in the trail. Watch the rocket's trail unfold: a tight circular loop near Earth (LEO), then a single elongated loop reaching outward (HEO checkout), then a long arc out to the Moon, a curved deflection near the lunar far side, and finally a return arc back into the Earth's vicinity. That figure-eight–ish shape is the visual signature of a free-return trajectory, and it's what makes Artemis 2 fundamentally different from a lunar-orbit mission like Apollo 8 or Artemis 3.
